Its imposing facade was obviously designed more as a climax to the street than as a face to the building: it became a paradigm for architecture of civic display. Julius's failure to realize either the palace-forum complex or the bridge led the next energetic urbanist, Paul III Farnese, to cut a street in 1543 from the Ponte San Angelo to Via Giulia to ease congestion (figs. lb, 3: no. 10). He chose to aim his street directly at the spot where the Florentines had been planning for years to build their national church. It now formed a V with the Via dei Banchi, and it appeared obvious to the Pope, who had realized the epoch-making trivium at Piazza del Popolo, that another street had to be cut to produce a trivium here as well. So, to match the new Via Paola, the Via del Panico was created. It had little purpose other than to balance the composition when seen from the Piazza del Ponte. It even fizzles out once it gets out of sight, but a contemporary fresco shows how impressive it was. It reminds us that the same Pope had Michelangelo plan the functionless left-hand palace on the Capitoline Hill in the interest of symmetry alone. Paul was always ready to let practical decisions be outweighed by aesthetic ones. (The Campidoglio of Michelangelo was the rnost splendid testimony to that inclination.
