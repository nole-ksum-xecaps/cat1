We do know that the Etruscans spread to the south from their original home in what is today Tuscany and conquered Latin populations that had migrated into the region in the previous two or so centuries. The records of the early period of Rome are woefully inadequate—some archeological evidence and later accounts based on oral traditions—but modern historians think the settlements on the hills of the area were united under Etruscan kings in the 8th century B.C.E. The Romans claimed that seven kings ruled the city from 753 B.C.E., when legend said that the city was founded by Romulus, until the aristocrats of the city expelled the kings and established a republic in 509. Historians regard Romulus as a mythical figure and are not certain that the next two or three kings existed, but they are confident that three Etruscan kings ruled the city in the 6th century, down to the revolution that later historians dated to 509. It appears that the Etruscan rulers adopted Latin, but historians attribute some components of Roman culture to Etruscan influence.The Roman Republic was an oligarchy, ruled by a small group of noble families that had always regarded themselves as patricians (fathers of their community) and as equals of the kings. In the mid-5th century, the people of the town, the plebs, rose against this small group and forced it to institute changes that transformed the politics of the state. The officers of the state were elected by the assembled citizens, and this assembly also had the power to legislate. In practice, the nobility used its wealth and social standing to organize the politics of the city around themselves. The nobles became the protectors—legal and economic—of people who relied on them, and they could rely on the votes of those they patronized.Beginning in the 4th century, Rome began to conquer Italy and by the mid-3rd century the city controlled the peninsula, including the ancient Greek colonies in the south. It then turned its attention to the Mediterranean. Rome faces west, and it was the western Mediterranean that first attracted the attention of the Romans. This region was then dominated by the Carthaginians of North Africa. (Carthage is in the modern country of Tunis.) These people had been colonists from Phoenicia—first arriving in the 9th century B.C.E.—and by the 7th century they controlled southern France, Spain, Sicily, Corsica, and Sardinia. Rome fought three wars against Carthage—the Punic Wars (from the Latin name for the Carthaginians)—264-241, 218-201, and 149-146. By the end of the second war, Rome controlled the whole western Mediterranean. The third Punic War was about destroying Carthage, only a specter of its former self, but a symbol of a time when Rome did not rule the West. Meanwhile, Rome’s influence in Greece grew steadily from the end of the 3rd century on. The Greek states had been at war with one another and had been forming leagues and confederacies since the 5th century. In the 4th century, the Macedonians had gained control, which gave the young ruler Alexander (r. 336-323) the foundation to conquer a vast empire stretching from Greece to India, including Asia Minor, Persia, and Egypt. After his death at 33, the Greek states fell into conflict again and again formed shifting alliances with one another and any other power that could help.
