This phenomenon is due partly to the strength of custom, partly tel the absence of radical social and economic change, and partly to the consistent elements in papal policy. What earlier Renaissance Rome contributed to the more comprehensive planning schemes of Sixtus V, and through him to all of Baroque Europe, was the emphasis on communication links rather than on spaces and monuments. Renaissance Rome is to be understood in its streets. Open spaces occurred where streets came together. But unlike later piazze those spaces, with the exception of the Campidoglio, were not aestheticized and were not used to purvey ideology, even though the designers may have in-tended they should be; they were left simply as open areas for gathering. It was the achievement of later generations to adorn them with fountains, obelisks, and columns.
