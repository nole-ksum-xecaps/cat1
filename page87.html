The Planning of Renaissance Rome, 1450-1580 JAMES S. ACKERMAN A CITY Is NOT JUST A PLACE where many people gather for social and economic interchange: it is a symbol, an expression of the unique way of life that takes shape in a particular setting. The history of a city can thus be read in its forms. An epigram favored by humanist writers: "Quanta Roma fuit ipsa ruina docet," can be applied to its Renaissance and Baroque re-mains as well as to its ancient ruins. But this heritage is usually experienced in terms of monuments -the Colosseum, the Basilica of St. Peter-and not as an integrated whole. I want to present Renaissance Rome rather as an organism evolving through the disposition of its parts, the conformation of streets, open spaces and built blocks, nurtured by popes and cardinals who, though motivated primarily by self-interest, were impelled by the stronger forces of cultural tradition and social needs to conform to a coherent evolutionary process. Most studies of Roman Renaissance planning focus on the program that Sixtus V carried out in the late 1580s, which introduced a new era of large-scale urban design. But the Sistine plan was only the culmination of a cen-tury and a half of less comprehensive interventions intended to open up and to expand the medieval city, to ease communications and to celebrate the papacy and individual popes and cardinals. Rome was the only great Italian city that was shaped in the Renaissance. Florence, Venice, Milan, Genoa, Bologna and Naples assumedtheir modern forms in the communal period of the later middle ages and most subsequent urban planning was confined to adjustments of those forms. But the exile of the Papacy in France during the fourteenth century left Rome with a depleted population, a ruined economy and no effective government. The popes returned to a small, decaying town of 17,000 inhabitants liv-ing in huts, lean-to's and hovels scattered about in disorder among the ruins of antiquity. There were no publically maintained streets; mud and gar-bage made vehicular traffic virtually impossible.
