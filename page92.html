Roman streets had been lined with porticoes before this time, and their suppression represented a radical change in the look of the city. It was prompted by the advice of King Ferrante of Naples, who is reported by a contemporary chronicler to have told the Pope: "You are not signore of your region and cannot be because you love porticoes and narrow streets. You must remove the porticoes and widen the streets and pull down the overhangs." Sixtus did so, the writer continues, "claiming it was for beauty." Sixtus, who left more inscriptions praising his public works than any other Renaissance pope, focused on functional improvements (fig. 4). The inscription under the famous fresco by Melozzo da Forli showing the Pope inaugurating the Vatican Library was probably written by the Librarian Platina himself, but he nevertheless felt constrained to give the planning program top billing. You gave your city temples, streets, squares, fortifications, bridges and restored the Aqua Virgine as far as the Trevi: you managed to give the sailors a port ... and to girdle the Vatican with a Sistine yoke. The city owes you even more: now the Library that had lapsed into squalor is exhibited in this famed place. A plaque originally located in the Via dei Pellegrini, which it calls Via Florea, tells how the health-bringing Sixtus has cleaned and ordered a filthy stinking mess. Julius II, the nephew of Sixtus, who appears in the library scene as Cardinal Giuliano della Rovere, carried on his uncle's initiatives. An inscription in the Via de' Banchi, which leads into town from Ponte San Angelo, reads: To Julius II, Pontifex Maximus, who, after enlarging the boundaries of the Papal State and freeing Italy, adorned the city of Rome, which had formerly been like an occupied city rather than a well-arranged one, with fine streets which he measured and widened in accordance with the majesty of the Empire. In a recent paper, Brian Horrigan pointed out that the reference to the majesty of the Empire, which is typical of Julius's bravado, is a quotation from Suetonius, and that the more common topos of squalor transformed into splendor is also drawn from antique precedents. These selections serve to indicate the direction of papal policy throughout the period.
