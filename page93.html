That the power was wielded partly in the interests of the public and partly in the interests of the Holy See will become clearer as we examine specific projects. Most undertakings during the first century of Renaissance planning in Rome were associated with one of three goals: to improve communication between the Vatican and the city; to develop and protect the Vatican Borgo itself; and to open passages from the northern entrance gate, the Porta del Popolo, into the city core. Instead of the grid within which Renaissance architectural theorists organized their streets and squares, Roman planners identified three disconnected focal points from which streets could radiate: the Vatican palace, the Ponte San Angelo, and the Porta del Popolo. In time, the systems met and intersected (figs. 1, 3, 9). The planning policy was selective, restricted to the priorities of the Holy See. Nothing was done to alter the medieval clutter of Trastevere. No routes into the city from the east, south, or west were developed during the first century of our period. The emphasis on the northern access route to the exclusion of others is attributable to the simple geographical fact that the overwhelming majority of the population of Italy and of Europe was settled somewhere north of Rome. The astonishing influx of pilgrims in Jubilee years-an influx which amounted to many times the size of the indigenous population-constituted a flood that had to be effectively contained and distributed. The importance of Sixtus IV della Rovere, pope from 1471 to 1484, in setting urban policy is inestimable; his achievements surpassed even the unfulfilled dreams of his predecessor, Nicholas V. The imminence of the Holy Year 1475 generated furious activity early in the pontificate-unless it should be put that the desire for furious activity generated the Holy Year, for, prior to this time, holy years had been fixed at intervals of a century and then of half a century. Sixtus formulated or confirmed all three of the priorities I have listed. He reconstructed nearly forty churches, founded seven new ones, and launched the symbolic program of the Capitoline Hill with a gift of famous statues. He also built the first modern bridge over the Tiber (figs. la, lb, 5: no. 1). During the 1450 Jubilee, over one hundred people had been killed in the crush that occurred on the Ponte San Angelo as pilgrims swarmed in and out of the Borgo. Sixtus's new bridge was to facilitate access to the Vatican. Its location was suggested by the existence of ancient foundations, but it also underscored the pope's personal policy by improving access to Trastevere, the locus of the community of the pope's countrymen, the Genoese, and by leading to the livestock market in the Campo dei Fiori (figs. lb, 5: no. 2), where his cardinal-nephew had built a huge palace, later called the Cancelleria.
