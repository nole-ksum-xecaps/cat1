Introduction to Pinkney, Napoleon III and the Rebuilding of ParisStanley ChodorowPinkney’s book presents a challenge, because he assumed that readers would know Paris well enough to know all the roads and places he mentions. I suspect that even readers who have lived all their lives in the city would have some difficulties, because they would not know the conditions that Haussmann altered in creating modern Paris. So, I have illustrated Pinkney’s book with maps I made. None of those maps are in the original publication.The maps in chapter 1, “Paris in 1850,” are based on a map of the city in that year. This map was inadequate for Haussmann’s work, so he had a better map made in 1853. But his predecessor as Prefect of the Seine, Jean Jacques Berger, had done some work after Napoleon became President of the Republic in 1851, so I thought that we should start with a map that represented the city before Napoleon started pushing for change. In any case, the map of 1853 no longer exists. It appears to have been destroyed when the Paris city hall burned in 1871.
