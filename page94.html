Sixtus's ambitions were complemented by a new charter for the Magistri Stratarum, the two noble citizens appointed to supervise the clearing and paving of streets and other urbanimprovements. A bull of 1480 gave them extraordinary powers to expropriate private property and to tax residents for enterprises affecting their neighborhood, whether or not they approved. A contemporary diarist tells us that when one Antonio Cenci protested the demolition of his house at the start of the trivium at Ponte San Angelo, he was not only thrown into jail but was also condemned to attend the demolition. The work on the city side of Ponte San Angelo, which the diarist called the trivium, did not basically alter the street pattern but rather improved three existing streets (fig. 4). The first on the left after crossing the bridge was already straight because it had survived from ancient Rome: the Via Reeta, soon renamed Via dei Coronari (figs. 3, 4: no. 4). The central one was the Medieval Via Papalis, through which papal processions passed to the Capitoline Hill, to the Lateran, and to Santa Maria Maggiore (figs. 3, 4: no 5). The street on the right, nearest the Tiber, was the Via Perigrinorum or Mercatoria (figs. 3, 4: no. 6), which led through the hotel and commercial area to the Campo dei Fiori and to the connector from Ponte Sisto. The urban projects of the succeeding popes all related in some way to the Sistine program. In preparation for the Holy Year 1500, Alexander VI Borgia fixed the future character of the Bargo by making a street parallel to the existing Bargo Vecchio, straight from the gate of Castel San Angelo to the gate of the palace (figs. lb, 3: no. 7). The Via Alessandrina or Bargo Nuovo had been envisaged by Nicholas V, and, as Alan Ceen points out in his indispensable thesis on the Banchi area, became the first leg of the Via Papalis. In the early years of the new century the Bargo Nuovo was the site of a number of palaces -built for members of the court like the Branconio dell' Aquila-that established a new type of urban domestic architecture. Julius II was as much of an urban propagandist as his uncle. He planned to span the Tiber with another bridge parallel to the Ponte San Angelo and to cut a major thoroughfare from its terminus on the city side to the terminus of the Ponte Sisto. The bridge was never built, but the Via Giulia became the widest and most splendid thoroughfare in the city (figs. lb, 3: no. 8; fig. 6). Here the pope started to build his Palazzo dei Tribunali, designed by Bramante (imposing fragments of the ground floor are still visible), and to open up before it a 50 x 100 meter piazza to link the new hall of justice to the old Cancelleria. The Via Giulia, thanks to its proximity to the river and to the presence of the Florentine community, attracted patrons of elegant palaces. Alessandro Farnese, who later became Pope Paul III, started the family palace in 1518 with its garden wall along the Via Giulia.
