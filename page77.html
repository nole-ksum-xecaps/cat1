So, the lords made sure that bishops and abbots of wealthy monasteries were their men. The rulers were founders and protectors of the churches and monasteries and asserted their right to choose the institutions’ leaders. Consequently, by the mid-10th century the clergy and a large proportion of the monastic community were not spiritual men chosen for their religious aptitude and interests. Many bishops led their contingents of troops into the field when they joined royal or ducal armies on campaigns. The reformers wanted to reform the character of the clergy and of the corrupted monastic communities.They developed ideas about the education, moral character, and proper aims of the clergy, and, eventually, they recognized that the only way to put men of the right sort into the leadership of the church was to change the way bishops and abbots were appointed. By the early 11th century, the reformers were calling for bishops to be elected by the clergy of their dioceses and for abbots to be elected by the monks of their houses. In Henry III of Germany(1038-56) they found a champion with the power to effect change. By 1046, Henry had achieved enough power and prestige to seek coronation as emperor. He led an army to Rome to receive the crown from the pope, but, when he got there, he found three popes. Although he supported the reformers, he asserted his power over the church. He called a council of bishops and had it depose all three popes. He then arranged for the council to elect his relativeto the papal throne. The new pope, Clement II, crowned Henry emperor, and Henry went back to Germany.Pope Clement lasted a very short time. Historians think he was poisoned by one of the local factions, but the disposal of this first “reform” pope did not end the threat to the local powers. Henry returned and installed another relative, who took the name Leo IX. Leo was a committed reformer, but he was also realistic about exercising power over Rome. He quickly left the city and started holding councils of bishops around Europe. In these councils, he enacted the reform agenda, centered on establishing the independence of the church through ecclesiastical elections. The reformers had come to the view that if they could free the church from the control of kings and great lords, they could reconstitute the clergy with educated, celibate men who had the spiritual interests and character to lead all Christians to salvation.For Roman history, the most important part of the reform legislation was that having to do with the election of the pope. In 1059, a council in Rome promulgated a new way to establish a legitimate pope; he would be elected by the cardinals, who would form an electoral college for the purpose. The term cardinal had once been applied to any priest who had the right to say Mass in a church of his diocese, a right granted by the bishop. By the mid-11thcentury, only Rome still used the term, and there was a set body of cardinals in the Roman diocese. The attempt to enforce this legislation and to enforce similar legislation about the election of bishops throughout the church led to a conflict between the church and secular rulers that lasted from the 1070s until 1122, when a settlement was reached. Election by clergy (or, in the case of the pope, election by the Roman cardinals) became the accepted regime, but after the election the local or royal patron of the church was asked for his approval.
