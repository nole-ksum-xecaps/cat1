Moreover, while they ruled Rome, they also ruled the Papal States, a quite different polity, and not at all like the contado of Florence or the terraferma of Venice. They headed, finally, the spiritual com-munity in all of Christendom for at least part of the period. Rome, conse-quently, could occupy only part of their attention and could provide only part of their income. They regarded it more as the seat of a wider realm than as an autonomous political unit, and they treated it partly as an ob-ject of exploitation and partly as a theatre. The physical and social conditions I have described do not by themselves make a city. They were the framework within which decisions had to be made, decisions that were motivated primarily by the ideology and ambi-tions of those in power. But if the urban policy of the Vatican was self-serving, it was not venal: the important interventions were not made for the sake of profit, and they almost invariably improved the city functionally and aesthetically, if often at the expense of the rights of the citizens. The goals of papal projects often were explicitly stated in inscriptions, bulls, and other official documents. The first significant instance of this is in the pages of .Manetti's Life of Nicholas V where a grandiose project of the 1450s is described for the remodeling of the Bargo, proposing three parallel arcaded streets leading to a great piazza with the Vatican obelisk placed at the center, anticipating by 130 years Fontana's feat of transpor-ting the obelisk from a spot south of the Basilica. The biographer also record-ed the Pope's deathbed speech in which he says: The great authority of the Roman Church can above all be understood only by those who have examined its origins and development through the study of texts. But the mass of the population is ignorant of literary matters and lacking in any culture. It still needs to be struck by gran-diose spectacles because otherwise its faith, supported as it is on weak and unstable foundations, will end in due time by declining to nothing. With magnificent buildings, on the other hand, monuments in some sense perpetual that appear almost to testify to the hand of God himself, the popular conviction may be strengthened and confirmed in the same way as it is in the affirmations of the learned. Nicholas goes on to say that the fortification of the Vatican is equally important. A few other statements will serve to illustrate the goals of papal plann-ing.
