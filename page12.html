The map represents the city on its hills and valleys. The Servian Wall was built in the 4th century B.C.E. after the Celts attacked Rome in 390. Only the Palatine Hill resisted the invaders; the rest of the city was pillaged and largely destroyed. The Campus Martius was a large flat area outside the city used to muster and drill troops. When the population grew too large for it to gather in the forum, the Campus was also used for the assemblies of the citizens in which theyelected magistrates. In ancient times, the Vatican was not a place but a hill. A road ran through the area leading away from the city toward the coast.The Tiber was in its present course and size by the time human beings arrived in the area. The first settlement was on the Palatine, which had a small village and primitive defensive walls. The settlement dates from about 1000 B.C.E. The settlers of this hill-village were Latins, one of the many branches of the Indo-European peoples, who included the Greeks, the Persians, the Aryans (who conquered northern India about 1000 B.C.E.), the Celts, Germans, and Slavs. So, early Rome was part of the great migrations of the Indo-European peoples, who originated in Central Asia, north of the Caucasus Mountains (modern Georgia and Armenia).Water was plentiful in the area of the seven hills, too plentiful. There were 23 springs on and around the hills, and the drainage from rain ran down the valleys between the hills and, mostly, sat on the low ground between the hills (the light- colored areas in the map above). So, while the hills provided good solid ground for building houses and other buildings, the low lands between them were swampy. Malaria was a big problem in the summer months.The inhabitants did not wait long to deal with this problem at a basic level. They dug ditches— cloacae = open sewers—that channeled rain water and drained the land near them. But the city had a serious malaria problem until the 13th century, when the popes—then the rulers of the city—built large-scale drainage systems.The water available for use by the population had become inadequate by the later 4thcentury B.C.E., so the city government began to build aqueducts to bring water into the city from springs and rivers in the surrounding hills. The first aqueduct was completed in 312 B.C.E. Another was added in the 270s B.C.E., and others were added as needed, usually after the need had become acute, of course.By the 8th century B.C.E. the Etruscans had moved into Rome. The origins of the Etruscans were long in dispute. Some historians have argued that they were a native Italian population, eventually sharing the peninsula with Greek settlers in the south and Latins, such as Romans. For the past 75 years, historians have thought that the Etruscans came to Italy from Asia Minor, the region that is now Turkey, in the 8th century B.C.E. Recently, DNA studies of goats in Tuscany (the name derives from Etruscan) have shown that they came from Asia Minor. The people brought the goats. The Etruscans eventually adopted the Phoenician alphabet, and we have many inscriptions from their towns in Tuscany and the Po River Valley, but no one has deciphered their texts.
