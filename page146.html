The Farmers General were the tax collectors of the regime, and their wall was built for tax purposes. At every entrance point, the tax men collected an excise tax on goods being brought into Paris. The wall had no military purpose. The outside wall, of dull green, was a defensive wall built between 1841 and 1844 by Prime Minister Adolphe Thiers under the restored monarchy of Louise-Phillippe (1830-50). This wall soon became useless, because military technology and tactics made it so. It was demolished in 1919 and eventually replaced by a ring road, the Boulevard Periphique, which marks the boundary of modern Paris.
