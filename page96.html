In the fifteenth century, the route from this point into Rome led along the ancient Cardo, called Via Lata. There was no need for more communication because the whole Campo Marzio area lying between the . gate and the center was sparsely populated and undeveloped. But as the population began to grow, Leo X saw the chance both to develop the area systematically and to call attention to the Medici family. His idea was to construct a second street out from the piazza running along the river and continuing to the edge of Piazza Navona (fig. lb: no. 13). The city terminus of Via Leonina was to be a piazza fronting the family palace of the Pope himself, which had been planned since his election. The palace project was dropped because Leo ceded the plot to Francis I of France as part of a political pact. Part of this land was later used for the French national church, San Luigi dei Francesi. Antonio Da Sangallo the Younger and Raphael were involved in the plan as advisers to the Maestri di Strada and, incidentally, as tenants on the new street. Antonio designed, in competition with Peruzzi, the large Ospedale degli Incurabili in the trapezoid between the old and the new streets. It was probably Raphael who first envisaged the third street of the trivium and, in addition, the erection of an obelisk at the center of the Piazza, projects realized only twenty and seventy years later respectively. Progress on the trivium, halted by the Sack of Rome in 1527, was deferred until 1538, when Paul III improved the Via Lata (later renamed Via del Corso after the races run there at festival time) and first realized the trident design by laying out the Via Paolina, the present Via del Babuino (fig. 1: no. 14). The inestimable influence of this concept in the history of planning may be traced to the Piazza del Popolo. Paul's development of the area was capped by the creation of a cross-axis that linked all three of the radiating streets with the Vatican: the Via Trinitatis, going from the Piazza di Spagna through today's Via Condotti and running parallel to the river at its bend, was the bond that brought the newest avenue into the preexisting network (fig. 1: no. 15). The last important project prior to the more dramatic era of Sixtus V already anticipated the Sistine vision. Via Pia, designed for Pius IV by Michelangelo, extended through an area of great villas and orchards from the colossal ancient statues ofthe Horse Tamers and the Quirinal Hill to the gate in the ancient walls giving access to Via Nomentana, the route east into the Abruzzi (fig. 10).
