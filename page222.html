Introduction to Caro’s The Power Broker: Robert Moses and the Fall of New YorkStanley ChodorowThe chapters drawn from Caro’s book present a common challenge for serious readers. He presents a clear, detailed picture of Robert Moses that drives the reader forward and seems, as you read it, to be complete and based on meticulous research in a wide variety of sources. The book, published in 1974, won a Pulitzer Prize. Reviewers and other readers extolled it as a definitive account of how Moses, Commissioner of Parks and Roads in New York City from the early 1930s to the mid-1960s, transformed the city. Time and change have altered the appraisal of the book and produced a view of Moses more nuanced than Caro’s.When reading a work like Caro’s, it is wise to pay attention to the time when it was written. In October 1975, New York City came within a couple of hours of going bankrupt. The city was saved by a reluctant teachers’ union, which used its pension fund to lend the city the money it needed to meet the debt obligations due on October 17 of that year. The federal government, under President Gerald Ford, refused to help the city on the grounds that it was responsible for its troubles. The president and people across the country regarded the city’s political leadership as corrupt and incompetent. Their profligate spending had created the crisis, and they should find a way to solve it.But a bankrupt New York would have affected people and institutions all over the country and beyond. The defaults on loans would have brought down banks across the country and even some international banks. The shutdown of non-essential activities would have darkened the country’s premier cultural spaces and institutions—the great museums, the Metropolitan Opera, the New York Philharmonic, and more. New York was the premier urban center in the country; damaging it seriously would have damaged our whole culture. The city was also the center of the American economy, which would also have been significantly affected by the city’s bankruptcy.Caro published his book just before the crisis of 1975. In the early 1970s, when he was completing the work, it was clear that the city was heading for trouble. That is the framework within which he wrote The Power Broker. His view of Robert Moses and his works was deeply affected by his view that Moses had accomplished the transformation of the city by doing an end-run around its political authorities. The money he invested in his projects did not come from taxes and therefore was not subject to the normal push-pull of politics. And politics represented the views of New Yorkers. So, Moses managed to climb above the fray of city politics and to carry out his plans with little or no regard for what the population of the city thought or wanted. One of the questions raised by such an assessment was, “How did Moses do it?” and Caro focused on the man’s personality, his elitist background and attitudes, his disdain for democracy.In The Atlantic piece by Cleveland Rogers, published in 1939, you will see an early assessment of Moses’ achievements.
