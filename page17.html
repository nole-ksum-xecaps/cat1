During this period, no emperor died in his bed.The wars were brought to an end by Diocletian, who won power in 284 and managed to unify military leadership in himself. He then set out to follow Augustus’ example to restore the empire’s culture as well as its peace. He reformed the government, establishing two emperors, one in the east (him) and one in the west (Maximian), because he recognized that the empire was too large and its problems too widespread to be governed by one person from one capital. He established his capital at Nicomedia, now Izmit in western Turkey. He also established that each Augustus should appoint a Caesar as his second-in-command and successor and that the Caesars should not be members of the Augustus’ families.Culturally, Diocletian sought to restore Roman mores—ethical government, a practical approach to challenges—and its religion, which he saw as the foundation of personal and social values. To understand what this religious restoration meant one has to understand how the civil wars had affected Roman government and society beyond the battles that brought one emperor after another to power.We think of the Roman Empire as a centrally governed state with the emperor and his bureaucracy managing everything. In fact, cities—municipia—carried the main burdens of government throughout the empire. The cities were corporations, led by councils of their leading citizens, that were responsible for maintaining the roads, bridges, aqueducts, and other public facilities in their territories. They collected the taxes, both in money and goods, for the central government. Most of the taxes went to pay for the vast army that protected the empire on its frontiers. The city councils were responsible for the taxes. If they did not meet the demand, they had to make up the deficit from their own funds.During the civil wars of the 3rd century, the competing armies greatly increased the demands for taxes, and the council members found themselves threatened with ruin. The result was that in a very high percentage of cities the councils resigned and retreated to their estates in the vicinity of their cities. The effect of the wars was thus to dismantle the deep level of Roman government as well as to create an unstable imperial leadership.Of course, the cities continued to need governments, not only to keep their infrastructure in repair but also to maintain the pagan cults. The priests who ran these cults came from the same families as those that controlled the councils. Indeed, the leading citizens of a town were often also the chief priests of the temples. So, traditional Roman religion also faltered during the civil wars. Moreover, the city governments had to take care of residents who fell into poverty, and the wars increased the number of poor people.After the collapse of municipal governments, the only organizations able to maintain even a modicum of social function were the Christian churches, which had by then an established leadership in its bishops and clergy.
