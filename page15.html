In fact, Octavian dominated every aspect of government. He replenished the Senate with his supporters and controlled elections to office. A few years after he won the civil war, the Senate made him Princeps and gave him the honorary title Augustus. The Princeps was an ancient position in the Senate that had traditionally been held by the most distinguished of the elder statemen. The Princeps had the right to speak first on any issue brought before the house. Once Augustus became Princeps, he defined the way every issue was discussed and decided, his honorary status was decisively enhanced by his control of the army. Augustus was the commander-in-chief, the Imperator (from which the title Emperor derives). In the past, this position had been temporary, as all service in the military leadership was, but Augustus made it permanent.Aside from his “restoration of the Republic,” Augustus undertook to restore Roman society. The aristocracy and other wealthy classes had become devoted to conspicuous consumption—wearing gold embroidered clothes, having dinners of peacock tongues etc.—and Augustus set out to restore the austere Roman way of life. He had sumptuary laws passed; he encouraged women to have children and give up the party circuit for motherhood; he promoted the idea that Rome ruled because it was worthy of the task and that its rule was ethical. But, while he wanted people to dress and behave in plain and practical ways, he wanted the city to look like the capital of the world. He undertook some building projects in the forum and nearby, but his greatest contribution to the city was to face its central buildings—basilicas and temples—in marble. The gleaming city we imagine when we visit the ancient part of Rome was originally the product of Augustus’s ambitions. By Augustus’ time, Rome had nearly a million people in it. The majority of the population lived in tenements, 4-5 story blocks with shops on the ground floor and apartments above. The higher one went in these buildings, the poorer and more cramped the living quarters became. Most of the roads of the city were unpaved and narrow. The nobility lived in spacious houses in certain sectors of the city, but their residences were accessible to the population, because people came every day to pay their respects and to ask for favors and support. However, under the empire, those old social, economic, and political relationships were not as important as they had been under the Republic. The emperor controlled the government, and the magistrates and leading political figures were his men and did his bidding.In this map, you can see the main buildings of the Forum Romanum, the ancient center of government and religion and, in gray outline, the tenements that ran up the hills and housed the population of the city. The “Forum Augusti” and “Forum Trajani” were centers built by those emperors (Augustus, 30 B.C.E.-14 C.E.; Trajan, 98-117 C.E.) to expand the governmental and commercial capacities of the city. The Amphitheatrum Flavium is today called the Colosseum. It was built in the 70s C.E., after the defeat of the Jewish rebellion (66-70). The Forum Romanum was where politicians engaged in their public campaigns.
