None of the water of Paris met the requirements. All of it contained chemical impurities that made it at least moderately hard. The waters of the Seine and the Ourcq were warm in the summer and excessively cold in the winter. They arrived in Paris turgid from clay in suspension and infected with organic matter, and the Seine picked up much more organic matter as it passed through the city. Except at the merchant fountains the city made no provision for filtering and, indeed, made no effort to purify the water. It was simply distributed as it arrived.37 In the first half of the nineteenth century Paris had suffered two fearful epidemics. Cholera, a pestilence unknown or un-identified in the West before the 183o's, had moved westward out of India in the preceding decade and descended on Europe in 1831. In Paris it attacked 39,000 persons and killed 18;400 them, including the Prime Minister himself. It struck again across Europe in 1848-49• and this time 19,000 Parisians died.
