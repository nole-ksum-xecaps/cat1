Rome was also unique in its social and economic situation. The power-ful members of the Roman aristocracy, families like the Colonna and the Orsini, were great feudal landholders who took no interest in the city. They retained residences, but not the elegant palaces designed by celebrated ar-chitects which rich Renaissance cardinals and papal bankers regarded as necessary to their image. There was a lesser and more modestly endowed nobility that filled the offices of civil government, and there was the Roman popolo which provided, to an overwhelming degree, services rather than goods. Rome had virtually no industry and exported nothing. Its popula-tion lived from serving the papal court and the pilgrims. A large part of it was impermanent; every papal election brought in a horde of job-seekers from the victor's home territory and prompted the departure of another horde that had enjoyed the support of the predecessor. This transient population weakened Romans politically, as they lacked the strong guild organizations built around industry and trade that were the core of civic enterprise in com-munal towns, and they lacked the income that might have provided a tax base for the expenses of government and the costs of corruption. Even if the Holy See had governed fairly and honestly, it would have been impell-ed to crush individual Romans with taxation. In fact, it was inspired vir-tually to encourage a thriving prostitution industry in order to levy on it heavy, piously-inspired taxes. Except for this last policy it was rather like Washington D.C. in its inability to function independently, its fluid popula-tion, and the weakness of its indigenous cultural life. Art, music, literature, science, humanistic scholarship-all had to be imported from abroad. There was no segregation of the poor from the rich; on the contrary, every quarter contained the full spectrum of the population: palaces and hovels stood side by side. The Cancelleria palace and others were built with street shops to be rented by small merchants, and Bramante and Raphael invented a new palace type with the ground floor wholly devoted to commerce and the single upper story to residential use. The building of the cardinals' palaces alongside the titular churches whenever practical, rather than in a wealthy quarter, also helped to spread the population out in an even mix of the rich and the poor. The most important segment of the population was the ruling group, the curial clergy. How can it be characterized? Not as a theocracy because it performed its secular functions without any religious coloration, just as if it had been a lay signory; not as an aristocracy or ruling class because the chief officers, the cardinals, were appointed. The ruler was elected by them from their membership and no posts were hereditary. He might come from a powerful and aristocratic family, or from humble and illiterate origins.
