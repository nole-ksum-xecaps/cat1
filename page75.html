Medieval RomeStanley ChodorowMedieval Rome was the product of the tribulations the city endured in the 6th century. Germanic tribes had been migrating into Italy since the early 5th century, when a large confederation of Visigoths took and pillaged the city (410 C.E.). The tribes did not find enough to keep them in Italy and continued their search for new lands. They first established a kingdom across southern France based on Toulouse and later migrated into Spain, where they established a kingdom based on Toledo.In Italy, the Visigoths were succeeded by the Ostrogoths, who in 489 migrated into the peninsula. They were led by Theodoric, whose Italian kingdom lasted until his death in 526. Shortly afterward, the Germanic kingdom was attacked by armies from Constantinople, the capital of the Eastern Roman Empire. The emperor Justinian (527-65) set out to reconquer the western provinces that various Germanic tribal confederations had seized. In 533, he sent his general Belisarius to reconquer North Africa, which he accomplished very quickly. He took Sicily in 535 and then turned his attention to Ostrogothic Italy, which had been in turmoil since the death of Theodoric.The combination of Belisarius’ operations and the rivalry among Theodoric’s successors created a complicated war in the peninsula, the so-called Gothic Wars. Rome was repeatedly attacked, heavily damaged, and pillaged by Gothic and Roman (Byzantine) forces over a period of more than twenty years. Aside from the warfare, the city, and the Italian population as a whole, were devasted by repeated plagues and natural disasters. The collapse of the governments of Italy resulted in the disintegration of the infrastructure that had kept disease under some control. During the wars, Rome’s population declined from about 1,000,000 to about 100,000.The result of the devastation and population loss was the abandonment of the ancient core of the city. The ancient buildings had been designed for a large population and required constant upkeep. The reduced population was not able to maintain the ancient structures, and the Forum Romanum ceased to be the center of the city. Instead, the population colonized the old Campus Martius, the relatively flat ground north of the classical city, building new tenements in the area. A market area, the Campo dei Fiori (Field of Flowers), became a new city center, and a few Roman facilities, such as the old race track, the Circus Flaminius, in the Campus Martius became centers of the displaced population.When Constantine left Rome for his new city of Constantinople (present-day Istanbul) in the 330s, he left an imperial administration behind in the ancient capital. The city was still the center of the cults of the Roman gods and of Roman culture. The imperial city government continued to appoint priests and professors of rhetoric who educated the aristocracy’s sons in arts that would serve them well in government positions.
