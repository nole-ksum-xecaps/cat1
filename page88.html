The ancient Rome on which the medieval city was built did not conform to what we know of Roman planning principles. 'The rectilinear order generated by two main streets crossing at right angles in the center was devised by ancient Roman administrators for new town settlements and could not be imposed on the sprawling and hilly capital with its sharply winding river. Towns such as Florence, Turin and Cologne (fig. 2) retained the Roman colonial form right up to modern times partly because it was functional and adaptable to medieval institutions both in form and scale and partly because the continuity of urban life in those places was not so destructively interrupted. Rome, however, differed from all other towns in topography, social organization and economy (fig. 1). * The depression occupied by the Renaissance city is surrounded on three sides by hills which were unsuited for dense habitation because of the lack of water. The ancients had brought water by aqueducts that ultimately fell into ruin. The Aqua Virgine was repaired first, in 1453, because the outlet was at one of the lowest points in the city and it could therefore be fed from a source that was only a few kilometers away, whereas the major ancient aqueducts originated in the higher elevations of the distant Alban Hills. The city remained in this hollow for over a century, until Sixtus V brought the Aqua Felice to the heights of the Esquiline and Quirinal hills, successfully promoting expan-sion in that direction. The river was a second major determinant of form because the layout of the streets had to be accomodated to its hairpin curve, and because its outlet in the Mediterranean made it a major source of supply. The location of the port on the left bank was the chief reason for the growth and significance of the Bargo of Trastevere (figs. 1, la, upper center). Medieval towns almost always spawned suburbs of this kind, which grew up on the far side of a wall or of a river at the point at which it was bridged. Rome had a second trans-Tiber Bargo at the Vatican (figs. 1, la upper right) that deeply affected the future growth of the city when the popes decided, after returning from Avignon, to settle there rather than at the Lateran Palace.
