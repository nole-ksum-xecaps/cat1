This came about when, with the collapse of the Empire, the Church, as the one centrally organiz-ed institution inherited its responsibilities for governance and protection, and bishops replaced provincial officers. The revival of the form in the Vatican Borgo symbolizes, perhaps unconsciously, the fact that the bishop of Rome, the Pope, like the early medieval bishop, was the city's chief tem-poral power and commander of the only protective force. For this reason, the Vatican Borgo was fortified, as soon as the Holy See could afford it, by Nicholas Vin the 1450s, while the city itself was open to attack through the holes in the Aurelian wall which, over the centuries, were repaired ex-clusively for antiquarian rather than for military reasons. The tomb of Hadrian on the Vatican side of the ancient Roman Ponte Elio was refashion-ed into a fortress to serve as the node of the defensive system. This use of ancient remains serves to introduce the impact of ancient topography on the formation of modern Rome (fig. 1). The Aurelian walls, which enclosed a city of almost a million in Imperial times, were so far from the focus of Renaissance life that vast open spaces intervened in which shepherds grazed their flocks and, in the sixteenth century, members of the Curia built their villas and vineyards. The walls affected the evolution of the modern city insofar as their gates, which opened onto major highways like the Appia, Nomentana and Flaminia, became the termini of major in-ternal avenues leading out from the center. Christian as well as pagan an-tiquities affected the communication network of the modern city. The great fourth-and fifth-century churches, official goals of pilgrimage, determined where major paths would be worn through the ruins and pastures. San Giovanni in Laterano, which remained the Cathedral even after the rebuilding of St. Peter, was by virtue of its unique status the goal of the Via Papalis, the principal route from the Vatican through the city. The authority of history also determined the eccentric position of the Campidoglio (figs. 11 la, center), the seat of secular government, where the Senator, Rome's Chief Justice and the city council, called Conservatori, did their business and the guilds had their offices. In other towns these func-tions were placed at the very center. But because the Campidoglio had been central in Rome's earliest times and had become the locus of medieval ad-ministration, Renaissance Romans were content to leave it on the borderline between the densely inhabited quarters and a wilderness of ruins. Its posi-tion revealed its peripheral political significance. Civic power was exercis-ed by the Vatican; the representatives of the people were figureheads who served to sustain a fiction of autonomy. The popes were careful, however, to richly endow the Campidoglio with sculptural and architectural sym-bols of the glory of the Roman people, restoring in fantasy what they preemp-ted in fact. Rome's lack of a single central piazza or core was one of the strongest determinants of its form.
