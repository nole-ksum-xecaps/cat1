The Germanic tribal confederations continued to pose a serious threat to the western provinces throughout this period, and the northern frontiers were the principal concern of the western branch of the imperial government.Meanwhile, the imperial government slowly turned Rome into a Christian city, and the result was that the imperial secular regime deteriorated. It had always managed both the secular and religious functions of government. The secular functions had moved to Milan, and the Christian church, led by the bishop of Rome, had taken over the religious functions. By the late 6th century, men educated to play roles in the imperial government were becoming bishops and taking over the governance of their cities. In Rome, the most outstanding representative of this trend was Gregory, who became bishop of Rome in 590 (d. 604).From the 7th century on, Rome was ruled by its bishop, who came to be called the pope, a title once used by all the bishops of the eastern empire (the title derives from the Greek for “father”). By the 7th century, the bishop of Rome was claiming exclusive right to the title and that eventually was accepted throughout the western Mediterranean. The eastern bishops began using other titles, such as Patriarch.In the 8th century, the pope found support from the leader of the Franks, the Germanic tribal federation that had taken control of western Europe in the 6th century. The close link between the pope and the Frankish king culminated in the coronation of Charles the Great (r. 768-814) as “Roman” emperor by Pope Leo III on Christmas day 800. The coronation confirmed the idea that the pope was the conveyor of the imperial title, and Leo crowned Charles’ son Louis as co-emperor in 813, the year before Charles died.The close relationship between pope and emperor lasted into the mid-9th century but weakened when the kingdom broke into Italian, French, and German realms after the death of Louis in 840. The result of the loss of royal support was that the Roman nobility gained increasing power in the city. By the late 10th century, the pope was one among several powerful “families” in the city, and the selection of each new pope resulted from a competition among these families. In the 1040s, this competition produced three competing popes, a low point in papal authority in the city and the church as a whole. In the meantime, during the 10th century, there arose a reform movement among monastic leaders in western Europe that sought to restore the proper qualities to the papacy and the church as a whole. As the papacy in Rome had fallen under the power of local families, so bishops and abbots throughout Europe had become the creatures of the great lords—kings, dukes, counts, and the like. The church had promoted the idea that salvation could be ensured by the prayers of holy men, even for those who lived decidedly unholy lives. Church doctrine insisted that sinners confess and seek absolution from the church before they died, but it also promoted the idea that the prayers of holy men would help such late penitents in the afterlife.
