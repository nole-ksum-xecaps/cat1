During the crisis of the 3rdcentury, the pace of its growth increased as people came to rely on it, especially for poor relief but also for social support that the pagan cults and the city governments had once provided. When Diocletian ended the civil wars and began his restoration of Roman society and culture, the church had become a prominent institution. The emperor regarded the church as a foreign organization preaching a radical monotheism that directly threatened Rome’s reliance on the pantheon of gods. Most of the Roman nobility and other leading citizens agreed with his view, and he viewed restoration of traditional Roman religion was one of the most important aspects of his general project to right the ship of the empire.Pagan religion was public and communal. The rites consisted in offerings to the gods made by the community as a whole for good harvests, victory in war, and the like. In the view of its leaders, everyone had to participate in the rites for them to be effective, because the gods were not being asked to give benefits to any particular person but to the whole community. The communal character of the religion made the political authorities responsible for its conduct, even though the various priesthoods carried out the rites. Christians refused to participate in the rites, and for most of the movement’s history up to the late 3rd century the authorities had ignored their absence. For Diocletian and his co-r ulers, determined to restore Roman society to its former glory, ignoring Christian resistance undermined their whole effort. So, they demanded that Christians participate and responded harshly when they failed to do so.Although, a few Christians, such as Stephen the Deacon, had died for their faith in the early period of Christian history, the new persecutions created new martyrs. There were not many of them, but their executions were public and gruesome, and the imperial government made sure that many people knew of them. So, after a period of about fifty years, when the Christian churches had gained members and status, they suddenly found themselves proscribed and under intense pressure to give up one of their core beliefs.Diocletian retired—a remarkable event in imperial political history—in 305, and he forced his co-Augustus in the west, Maximian, to retire as well. The Caesars, Galerius in the east and Constantius in the west, succeeded as Augusti. Diocletian’s system then broke down, because Constantius appointed his son Constantine as his Caesar. The two Augusti and then their Caesars were soon at war to unify the imperial rulership. In the west, Maxentius, son of Diocletian’s original co-ruler Maximian and son-in-law of his successor Galerius, seized Rome and declared himself the western Augustus. Constantine succeeded his father in 306, when the older man died, and opened a war against Maxentius. Constantine’s conquest of Rome in 312 is the beginning of our course. During the war, Constantine (r. 312-337) had converted to Christianity, and now he was ruler of the city that was the center of the empire’s pagan cults.
