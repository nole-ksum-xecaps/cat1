The incorporation of the Greek-speaking populations of the eastern Mediterranean had a profound effect on Roman culture. There were some in Rome who fought against the adoption of Greek culture, but they lost that battle. By the 2nd century B.C.E., educated Romans learned Greek and were creating a culture we call Greco-Roman. The Romans copied Greek sculpture, imported Greek religions, studied Greek philosophy, and memorized Greek literature, especially Homer. Roman theater, poetry, and art followed the Greek models and traditions. When the Romans began to write their history, they tried to make their political culture equal to the Greek. Athens had reformed its constitution at the end of the 6th century, which led to its great flowering in the 5th century. The Romans reconstructed their own history to equal or better the Athenians’. They dated the foundation of the Republic to 509, a little before the Athenian revolution.As Rome’s empire grew, wealth and people poured into the city. Romans built basilicas to house government offices, courts, and, eventually, shops. These buildings were in the forum or near it. They were large box-like buildings with big halls and many entrances. Rome also served as the religious capital of the empire. The forum contained many imposing temples and monuments to victories and the heroes who won them. But the Romans took pride in beingpractical and effective. Greece built gorgeous, decorated buildings; Rome built what it needed to run what its people regarded as the world. So, republican Rome was rich and impressive but plain.In the 1st century B.C.E., the political competition among the leaders of the Republic increased dramatically, because political positions would deliver wealth and power. The competition began at the end of the 2nd century, when two noblemen—Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus—used the popular assembly to gain power against their own class, and thereafter ambitious men worked hard to create popular parties to support their own ambitions. In addition, the empire produced many military challenges, so that military leadership also became an avenue to power. In the early 40s B.C.E., the competition led to civil war between Julius Caesar and Pompey, two great military leaders who had expanded the empire in the east (Pompey) and Gaul (modern France and Belgium—Caesar). Caesar won this war and had the Senate—the body of former republican magistrates and the agent of aristocratic power—declare him dictator. In old Roman law, the Senate could appoint a dictator to meet an emergency, and the dictator was supposed to step down as soon as the emergency passed. Caesar did not step down, and in March 44 a group of nobles assassinated him in the Senate House.The assassination led to a new civil war between those who had supported Caesar and the aristocratic, Senate faction that had killed him to restore their own control of the Republic. The aristocratic party was soon defeated, but then Caesar’s party broke in two and went to war with itself. The leaders of the new conflict were Marc Antony, Caesar’s right-hand-man, and Octavian, Caesar’s nephew. In 30, Octavian prevailed and proclaimed the restoration of the Republic.14
